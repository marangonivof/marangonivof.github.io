Marangoni-Driven Flow of Liquid Metal Under Localized Heating

A VOF-based numerical investigation of thermocapillary convection at a liquid aluminium–gas interface driven by Gaussian arc-like heating.

Saloni Kansal · Ali Fakhreddine · Mohamad Al Nasser · Magdalena Schatzl · Abdellah Kharicha

Explore Results Read Abstract

Abstract

Surface-tension gradients powering interfacial flow

When a plasma or electric arc interacts with a liquid metal surface, intense localized heating can generate strong interfacial temperature gradients and surface-tension-driven flow. In this work, simulations are carried out for a liquid aluminium–gas interface using a VOF-based numerical model to study thermocapillary flow under localized heating. A Gaussian heat source is applied near the interface to represent localized thermal heating caused by plasma or arc interaction. Temperature-dependent surface tension is included to capture Marangoni stresses.

The results indicate that, as the imposed heat flux increases, the reduction of surface tension near the heated region becomes more pronounced, leading to stronger Marangoni forcing along the liquid–gas interface. This enhances outward interfacial flow and promotes lateral heat redistribution.

Physical Mechanism

Three steps from arc to flow

⚡

Localized Arc Heating

A plasma or electric arc deposits energy over a concentrated region of the free surface, creating a non-uniform temperature field along the interface.

📉

Surface-Tension Gradient

Since surface tension in liquid aluminium decreases linearly with temperature, the hot central zone has lower σ than the cooler peripheral regions.

🌀

Marangoni Convection

The tangential stress imbalance drives fluid outward from the centre, forming two symmetric recirculation cells that redistribute heat laterally.

Conclusions

What the simulations tell us

  1. Peak temperature and minimum surface tension at the heated centre. The Gaussian heat source creates a pronounced thermal maximum where σ reaches its minimum, establishing the outward driving force.

  2. Two symmetric counter-rotating recirculation cells. Near-interface flow is directed outward; mass conservation is satisfied by a return flow deeper in the liquid.

  3. Higher heat flux → stronger Marangoni-driven motion. Velocity magnitudes scale with imposed heat flux because a larger thermal gradient produces a proportionally stronger surface-tension imbalance.

  4. Marangoni convection promotes lateral heat redistribution. Compared to conduction-only cases, thermocapillary flow broadens the temperature profile and reduces peak temperature — critical for accurate melt-pool modelling.
